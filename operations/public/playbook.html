1 The one idea
We help nonprofits prove their programs work — and use that proof to improve them and raise more funding.

Everything we sell solves one root problem: funding. Program data, evaluation, quality, reporting — that's the work, but it all exists to protect and grow the money that keeps good programs alive.

You don't sell that root directly. You sell the pain a prospect feels, and anchor it to funding. And every deal points toward one prize: a recurring retainer.

3 Who you sell to

Lean in

Small-to-mid nonprofits ($250K–$3M) with a program that has measurable outcomes and multiple funders who want reports. No full-time data person. Drowning in spreadsheets or paying for 3–5 tools. Strongest signals:

  • → "Reporting season is brutal here."
  • → "We know it works, we just can't prove it."
  • → "Our data lives in five places."
  • → Just landed a multi-year grant with real reporting.

Walk away

Disqualifying fast is a skill, not a failure.

  • ✕ Advocacy/arts orgs with no outcome data.
  • ✕ Orgs with a real data team and a system they love.
  • ✕ Price-only objection with zero reporting pain.
  • ✕ Anyone who really wants a CRM or donor database.

5 The two pitches

Route by felt need. Never blend them.

For the funding-insecure org
"You're doing real work, but fighting for every dollar — and the hardest part is you can't show a funder it's working, even though you know it is. That gap is costing you grants you've earned. Fix the proof, and the funding follows. We can start small: the software gets your data in one place for a few thousand a year, and from there we build the evidence that wins the next grant."
For the funding-secure org
"You've got the funding. What you don't have is your sanity at reporting season — data in five places, every report a lost weekend. We put it in one place so reporting takes an afternoon. And here's what surfaces once we do: the numbers you've been reporting don't actually show outcomes — which is exactly what funders are starting to ask for. We fix that too, so the renewals you're counting on don't quietly slip."

8 Discovery questions

Find the felt need, then route. One question per turn — let them talk. Name their pain back to them more clearly than they named it.

"Walk me through your last grant report — what did putting it together actually look like?"finds reporting pain
"When a funder asks if your program is working, what do you show them?"finds the proof gap
"Where does your program data live right now?"finds the five-places problem
"Is there anyone whose job is the data and evaluation?"finds the missing hire — your sweet spot
"What outcomes did you promise funders this year, and how confident are you that you can show them?"finds funding risk
"Is funding tight right now, or is the bigger pain keeping up with what you've got?"places them on the spectrum
9 Objection scripts

The page to flip to mid-call. They say → you say.

It's too expensive.
You say"Fair — let's add up what you already spend across tools and staff time at reporting season. Most orgs your size are already past this number; it's just spread out and invisible. And a lot of orgs fund this right out of a grant's evaluation line." (Then ask if their grants have an eval budget — they usually do.)
We track everything in spreadsheets.
You say"Spreadsheets are great until reporting season — then they're five files, three versions, and a weekend. The question isn't whether you can track it; it's what it costs you every time you have to report it."
We're too small for this.
You say"Small is exactly who this is for. Big orgs have a data team — you are the data team, on top of everything else. This gives a small org the reporting muscle of a big one, without the hire."
Can't the software do it without the consulting?
You say"It can hold and report your data beautifully — but only what you've defined, and most orgs haven't defined the right outcomes yet. That's the sprint. Start with just the software; most people want the sprint within a few months."
We already have a consultant.
You say"Great — then you have direction. The question is whether it's getting implemented and measured. I stay through implementation, and I bring software and an evaluation framework most consultants don't."
We don't have time right now.
You say"That's the point — this gives time back. Setup's on me, reporting stops eating weekends. If now's genuinely wrong, let's pick the season after your next big deadline."

The setup-waiver wedge

Software setup is waived when they bundle a consulting retainer. "If we're already working together, setup's on me." Makes the software an easy yes and pulls them to the retainer.

Sliding scale (equity pricing)

Under $500K → 20% off · $500K–$2M → 10% off. How you serve the orgs who feel the pain most but can pay least. It's values, not a discount gimmick.

11 The competitive story

Two stories. Use one — don't blend.

Story A — replace the stack

For better-funded orgs already overspending on Tableau + Submittable + StoryRaise + Typeform — $9–14K/yr of disconnected tools.

"One platform built for your program — replaces a $9–14K stack, starting at $3,000."

You win on price and simplicity. (The funding-secure end.)

Story B — stop duct-taping

For small orgs wiring together Airtable + Jotform + Looker for ~$1,200/yr. You are not cheaper — don't pretend to be.

"It doesn't replace a $1,200 line item — it replaces a part-time data hire you can't afford."

You win on time and expertise. (The funding-insecure end.)

vs. Clear Impact & other consultants

Don't attack Clear Impact — out-position. You're the modern, designed, human RBA platform: same framework funders trust, built for how programs actually work today. And unlike most consultants, you're a practitioner who stays through implementation — and brings software no consultant has.
